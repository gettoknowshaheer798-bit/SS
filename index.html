Chapter One

A Smart Monkey Named Shaheer

In which quiet intelligence saves the day

In a jungle, there once lived a smart monkey named Shaheer.

Shaheer was smart, but he never announced it. No speeches. No showing off. He was quiet, observant… and excellent at banana management.

When the jungle faced a crisis — the trees stopped giving fruit, the rivers stopped flowing, the grass turned pale and unhappy — everyone panicked. Drama everywhere. Every animal blamed someone else.

The giraffe blamed the soil, because he could never touch it with his long neck. The elephant blamed the caves, because he was too big to fit inside them. The goat blamed Sidra… because she was the GOAT.

Shaheer quietly climbed a tree and looked around. He noticed something small: ants were building a straight highway down into the roots of the trees. He gently moved them away, cleared the river, covered the soil with leaves. Then he waited.

A few days later, the trees gave fruit again.

The lion asked him: "Why didn't you tell us earlier?"

"Smart work doesn't need announcements."

Later that day, Shaheer walked up to the goat. "Sidra is the GOAT," he said. "That's a fact." The goat got angry: "I am the real goat!" Shaheer smiled: "No… GOAT means Greatest Of All Time." The goat paused. Then nodded, accepting his defeat.

Moral

Real intelligence is calm, kind, and doesn't need to show off.

Chapter Two

Small Chaos, Soft Hearts

Jerry the Giraffe and the Yogurt Disaster

Jerry the giraffe was very tall, very curious, and very hungry. He didn't only love leaves. He also loved yogurt. Big yogurt. Very big yogurt. Because bending his neck was not easy.

One bright morning, Jerry saw a picnic table at the edge of the jungle. On it sat the biggest, creamiest yogurt cup he had ever seen. His eyes went wide. He stretched his long neck toward it, tongue out, leaning from the branch overhead…

o Plop! o

His head went straight into the cup. Yogurt splashed everywhere: on his nose, on his ears, all along his skinny neck, in his eyelashes, and somehow on a bird three trees away.

He wiggled left. He wiggled right. He did a small, undignified shimmy.

o Pop! o

He came free, leaving a snowy trail of yogurt on the ground. The monkeys jumped down immediately and started licking it. Birds flew low, splashing tiny yogurt drops. The raven took notes. Three ants formed a committee to investigate.

"Oh no. I only wanted one bite… not a jungle party!"

By evening, Jerry lay under a tree, tired and happy, licking the last bit of yogurt off his nose. A butterfly had landed on his neck and appeared to be staying the night.

"Tomorrow," he whispered, "I'll try yogurt again… but maybe with a spoon."

The moon wrote a small note in its dream-delivery log: Jerry the Giraffe. Request: dreams about yogurt with adequate spoon access. Filed. Processing.

Chapter Three

Sidra's Games & Shaheer's Innocence

In which glitter is weaponised

There once lived a boy named Shaheer. He was possibly the most innocent human on the planet. He believed everyone was kind. Even vending machines that once ate his snacks.

Sidra, on the other hand, was a professional troublemaker.

It was a bright Wednesday morning. Sidra woke up with one mission: steal Shaheer's favorite pencil with the golden eraser, replace it with a pencil that writes upside down, and sprinkle glitter into his bag.

Glitter. The true agent of chaos. The thing that never leaves.

Soon, Shaheer opened his bag. Glitter exploded. It went everywhere: on his hands, his face, his uniform, in his hair. He looked like a sparkly snowman who had just celebrated something. Sidra silently clapped. This was going well.

Shaheer blinked. Then smiled: "Wow, Sidra! You made me glittery. I feel magical."

He tried the upside-down pencil. The words came out upside down. He tilted his notebook. Then he tilted further and started writing on purpose, upside down, and wrote an entire poem:

Roses are red,
Violets are blue,
Sidra plots mischief,
Shaheer smiles true.

Sidra felt her evil plans crack slightly in the middle.

Then came the whoopee cushion. Shaheer sat. A loud noise echoed through the classroom. He jumped up and clapped: "It sounds like a trumpet!" — but inside, he felt embarrassed. The laughter didn't stop. He lowered his head. He tried to keep the smile on his face. He could not.

And finally… he ran out of the classroom crying.

Sidra watched. This time… she had won. At the end of the day, Shaheer had more fans because of his kindness. And Sidra… had more evil ideas. And a small, invisible feeling she didn't have a name for yet.

Because even innocence has its limits.

"Real intelligence is calm, kind,
and doesn't need to show off."

— The Jungle, Chapter 1

Chapter Four

Playful Punishment and Unspoken Affection

In which one word means everything

Sidra ruled a tiny invisible kingdom made of rules and discipline.

Her punishments were calm. Precise. They arrived without warning and were carried out without drama, which is the most effective kind of punishment because the person receiving it has no idea what to do with themselves afterward.

"Shaheer. Punishment time."

"Double leg raises. Two minutes." Or planks — her favorite, the one she reached for most, because she had, by now, openly admitted that the punishing itself was half the fun.

Shaheer's legs would shake. His eyes would sting. Small tears would form and fall despite his best efforts to prevent them. Sidra watched silently. Not cruelly. Just clearly.

When it ended, she would walk past him — not stop, not look back — and say one word:

"Good."

That one word made everything worth it.

It wasn't the punishment that mattered. It was the single, calm, completely certain "Good" that followed. It meant she had noticed. It meant she believed he could do it. It meant he had.

Later, she whispered: "Go to sleep now… or more leg raises." Shaheer obeyed. And slept. And in his sleep, probably did more leg raises, because even asleep he was trying to impress her.

Sidra sat quietly in the dark. Not watching. Just present. Which is its own kind of punishment. The nice kind.

Chapter Five

When Silence Hurts More Than Pranks

In which Sunday feels very far away

Shaheer had just started a new job. A job that ate hours. A job that swallowed lunch breaks whole without chewing. A job that turned five minutes into meetings and meetings into hours and hours into weeks that arrived already half-finished.

And somewhere inside his phone, in the part of the phone that glowed most softly at night, lived Sidra.

Shaheer didn't just like her. He liked her too much for the situation he was now in. He could only send short messages. Tiny replies. "Okay." "Yeah." "Ha." Dry conversations. Conversations that sounded like they were happening through a wall.

Sidra had rules:

"I don't do small conversations. I will only talk on Sunday."

Shaheer read the message at work. Between two meetings. Under a fluorescent light that flickered in a way he now found personally offensive. And he felt very quiet inside. Not sad, exactly. Just quiet. The way a room feels quiet after someone has just left it.

That night, alone in his room, he whispered to no one:

"I don't need long talks…
I just need you for one minute."

But Sunday was far away. Four days far away. The week stretched between them like a very long, badly lit corridor.

Sidra held her phone that same night. She told herself she wasn't looking at the chat. She was just holding her phone. Her thumb hovered. She put it down. She picked it up again. She was very convincingly not looking at the chat.

Because she missed him too. But Sidra didn't move toward things she missed. She waited for them to come to her. This was either wisdom or stubbornness, and she had decided long ago not to investigate which.

Shaheer cried quietly, in the small, private way of someone who doesn't want to make a production of it. Not to complain. Not even to feel better. Just to wait. Just because waiting, sometimes, is the only thing left.

Chapter Six

The Night of the Half-Sentences

Not narrated. Lived.

In a city where messages arrived faster than footsteps, there lived two night-talkers. One was Shaheer, a boy who typed with too many words and too many hearts. The other was Sidra, a girl whose silence spoke louder than most people's speeches.

They didn't meet in streets. They met inside glowing screens… between typing… and seen.

When the sky softened near Fajr, Sidra sent him something invisible: a hug. Not in pixels. In intention.

"Imagine it," she wrote.

Shaheer smiled at his screen like an idiot. He held that hug in the only place he trusted: his chest.

And for the rest of that night, Shaheer lay still… holding an imaginary hug, and learning the hardest kind of silence: the one you choose.

Above the city, through one small window, Luma the cloud drifted past. She couldn't knock. She was a cloud. But she stayed there for a while, against the glass, in the lilac dark, because sometimes being present is the only tool you have. And sometimes it's enough.

"Maybe Shaheer was not cold.
Maybe he was simply not expressive."

Chapter Seven

The Night the Moon Lost Its Wi-Fi

In which dream delivery goes off-schedule

Something very serious happened. The moon's Wi-Fi stopped working. No one noticed. Except one boy. Shaheer.

He was lying on his bed, holding his phone like it might suddenly grow wings and fly back to Sidra. He refreshed. Nothing. He refreshed again. Still nothing. He put the phone down. He picked it up. He turned it off. He turned it on. He stared at the ceiling.

The moon blinked slowly above his window.

"Sorry. I accidentally put my router inside a cloud."

"I run the Night Department. Dream delivery. Soft thoughts. Random memories at 2:37 a.m. You know. Professional stuff."

"There is one girl," the moon said. "She ordered a very specific dream."

"Funny. Safe. Quiet. With no emotional damage. And at least one soft laugh."

The moon described her: "She is tired in a brave way. Her silence is organized. Her laughter is rare… but extremely dangerous when activated." The moon paused. "Also she has opinions about how tea should be made."

Shaheer smiled without trying. "I know."

And he wrote her dream: Sidra is walking through a tiny street made of soft clouds. There is a bakery. But instead of bread… they sell naps. Little wrapped naps. Strawberry naps. Extra-sleep naps. Five-minute emergency naps.

Behind the counter stands a giraffe. It is wearing small round glasses. It looks extremely professional. It also looks familiar, though Sidra can't quite place it.

"Welcome. Would you like your rest warm or slightly irresponsible?"

A monkey walks past. The monkey drops a banana. The monkey trips over the banana. The monkey apologizes to the banana. Then stands up, brushes itself off with great dignity, bows to Sidra, and says:

"Sorry. I'm still learning gravity."

Sidra covers her mouth. She laughs again. A little louder this time.

Shaheer finished writing. The moon read it slowly. Turned the page. Read it again. Then closed the notebook.

"That," the moon whispered, "is perfect."

The cloud-router blinked back on. Signal restored. Dream delivered.

The moon leaned closer through the window and said softly, "She smiled. Not big. But real."

Chapter Nine

The Biscuit Theory

In which Shaheer is disciplined. Repeatedly. (A Theorem in Several Stupid Parts)

Shaheer was doing something extremely serious. He was trying to balance a biscuit on top of his phone. On purpose. Because in his head, this made perfect sense.

"If it stays for ten seconds, Sidra will reply."

The biscuit fell in two seconds. He gasped. "Wow. Betrayal."

At the exact same time, Sidra was sitting straight on her bed, phone in her hand, back perfectly against the pillow. Her room was calm. Her blanket was folded. Her charging cable was neatly placed. Her expression was quiet. Focused. Dangerously peaceful.

She had already seen Shaheer typing… deleting… typing again. She waited. Because Sidra liked waiting. She was good at it. She had, at this point, made it into an art form.

Chapter Eight

The Lilac Cloud and Sidra's Secret Sentence

In which Luma supervises

Tonight, the sky was lilac. Not dramatic lilac. Just calm, end-of-day lilac. The colour that the sky becomes when it's tired but not unhappy. Her favourite colour, as it happens — though she'd never admit the sky was showing off for her.

A small cloud named Luma floated low over the jungle, quietly supervising emotional situations she was absolutely not trained for. She had, at this point, accumulated approximately forty-seven hours of field experience. Her qualifications remained unchanged: none.

On the same long branch, but at a very polite distance, sat Sidra and Shaheer. Earlier tonight, Shaheer had been mad. Not shouting. Not throwing bananas. Just… bad tone. The kind of bad tone that sounds small but lands heavy. Sidra had gotten mad because of him. So Sidra did what she always does when she's hurt. She went silent.

Sidra finally spoke: "Hold your hand out." "Why?" Shaheer asked. "Just do it," said Sidra, in the tone that closes arguments.

Shaheer obeyed. Sidra placed a small folded paper into his hand. He opened it.

"That sentence is not for explaining. It's for someone who knows how to sit quietly with another person… even when they're annoying."

Shaheer stared at the paper. He read it. He read it again. He tilted his head slightly. He looked up at Sidra. He looked back at the paper.

"Is this… a spell?" Sidra looked away, hiding a smile. "I wrote this once. And I actually know what it means."

Shaheer's head lifted very slowly. "You know the meaning?" "Yes." "Then tell me." Sidra smiled: "No."

Before leaving, Sidra turned back for half a second and said, very casually:

"By the way… I'm not silent anymore."

Shaheer froze. Then smiled. Because that sentence meant more to him than the meaning of any secret line ever could.

Above them, the lilac sky kept the paper's secret safe. For now.

Chapter Ten

Goodnight, Shaheer

A quiet coda

For the third night in a row, Shaheer waited for the same small word. Nothing. Sidra had just written: "Go. Sleep."

"Why don't you say goodnight to me anymore?"

The reply came quickly. "I won't now." His heart dropped. "…Why?" Seen. And then she left.

Shaheer kept staring at the chat, like the messages might suddenly change into something kinder. They didn't. He let out a small, broken laugh. It was funny in the saddest way — how one missing word could suddenly make the whole night feel heavier.

Sidra was the only person who truly noticed when he tried to fix himself. When he started praying again. When he tried to sleep better. When he chose silence over bad company. She never forced him. She just made him want to be better.

Shaheer didn't talk much with his family. Somehow, without realizing it, his life had shrunk into a very small circle. And inside that circle… there was only Sidra.

Maybe she's slowly drifting away.

He rubbed his eyes with the back of his hand. "Relax, Shaheer," he whispered, trying to joke. "You're really crying over a goodnight." But he knew it wasn't about a word. It was about losing the one person who made him feel like he wasn't wasting his life anymore.

He opened the chat again. This time, he didn't ask anything. He typed a small bedtime story for her:

Once there was a stubborn boy who stayed awake every night
because one special person made his world feel less heavy.
He wasn't perfect. He made bad choices before.
But when the girl entered his life,
he slowly learned how to choose better ones.

Even when she stopped saying goodnight,
he still wished peace for her before sleeping.

Because loving someone doesn't always mean being close…
sometimes it only means caring quietly.

He sent it. Shaheer placed his phone face down beside his pillow. He turned to his side.

Shaheer fell asleep still thinking about her — and about how someone could become your entire world without ever promising to stay.

"Some people are not loud comfort.
They are soft company.
Still counts."

— The Giraffe, Chapter 7
